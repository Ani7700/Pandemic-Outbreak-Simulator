Solvik: a short briefing

Solvik is a contagious childhood disease that a two-dose vaccine can prevent. The fewer children an area has vaccinated, the more freely it spreads. There is a tipping point called herd immunity, the level at which the disease can no longer sustain itself, and for Solvik that takes roughly 89% of children having had both doses.

Cambridgeshire is in a fairly comfortable position. Ninety per cent of its children have had the second dose and ninety-three per cent the first, which puts it just above that 89% mark. Only around twenty-eight cases have surfaced there in the past 6 months, few when set against the worst affected places, so its community risk is judged to be no more than medium.

Hackney reads very differently. Only seventy-one per cent of its children have completed the second dose, and eighty per cent the first, which leaves the borough well below the herd-immunity threshold. It has already recorded about eighty-five cases in the past 6 months, among the higher counts anywhere, and its community risk is rated very high. Of the two places, then, Cambridgeshire is the better vaccinated, while Hackney carries both the greater case load and, for an unvaccinated child, the greater chance of catching Solvik over a year.

Whether an exposed child actually falls ill turns largely on their own doses. A child with none who meets a case has something like a five in ten chance of catching it. A single dose pulls that down to about two in ten, and both doses bring it to only around five in a hundred. The first dose does the heaviest lifting, since the fall from five in ten to two in ten outweighs the further drop to five in a hundred. Even so, no amount of vaccination erases the danger completely, and after two doses a small chance always lingers. In round numbers, of two hundred unvaccinated children all exposed, roughly one hundred would be infected, against about five of every hundred who had had both doses.

Were an outbreak to take hold, it would not hold steady. Over the six weeks the modelling suggests the number infected would climb — quickly where vaccine coverage is thin, slowly where it is high — with the fastest-moving outbreaks reaching a high point inside that window and slower ones still rising when the six weeks are up. Any such projection carries a spread of possibilities around its most likely path, which is a measure of how uncertain it is rather than a single promised figure, and it describes what could happen rather than what is bound to. Where coverage is thinner, as in Hackney, that wave gathers pace far faster than in a better-protected place such as Cambridgeshire. In concrete terms, the modelling puts Hackney on a steady climb to around five and a half thousand people infected within the six weeks and still rising by the end, whereas in Cambridgeshire an outbreak barely takes hold at all, holding to no more than a few dozen.
