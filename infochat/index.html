Verrow fever: a short briefing

Verrow fever is a highly contagious childhood disease that a two-dose vaccine can prevent. The fewer children an area has vaccinated, the more freely it spreads. There is a tipping point called herd immunity, the level at which the disease can no longer sustain itself, and for Verrow fever that takes roughly 93% of children having had both doses.

Cambridgeshire’s picture is fairly reassuring, though not complete. Ninety-one per cent of its children have had the second dose and ninety-six per cent the first, which leaves it sitting just short of that 93% mark. Only around twenty cases have surfaced there in the past six months, few when set against the worst affected places, so the community is judged to be at no more than a medium level of risk.

Hackney reads very differently. Barely more than half of its children, fifty-six per cent, have completed the second dose, and sixty-four per cent the first, which leaves the borough far beneath the herd-immunity threshold. It has already recorded about a hundred and eleven cases in six months, among the heaviest anywhere, and its community risk is rated very high. Of the two places, then, Cambridgeshire is the better vaccinated, while Hackney carries both the greater case load and, for an unvaccinated child, the greater chance of catching Verrow fever over a year.

Whether an exposed child actually falls ill turns largely on their own doses. A child with none who meets a case has something like a six in ten chance of catching it. A single dose pulls that down to roughly three in ten, and both doses bring it to only about seven in a hundred. The first dose does the heaviest lifting, since the fall from six in ten to three in ten outweighs the further drop to seven in a hundred. Even so, no amount of vaccination erases the danger completely, and after two doses a small chance always lingers. In round numbers, of two hundred unvaccinated children all exposed, roughly a hundred and twenty would be infected, against about seven of every hundred who had had both doses.

Were an outbreak to take hold, it would not hold steady. Modelling suggests the number infected would climb over the opening months to a peak and then subside as the outbreak burned itself out, the whole arc playing out across something like half a year. Any such projection carries a spread of possibilities around its most likely path, which is a measure of how uncertain it is rather than a single promised figure, and it describes what could happen rather than what is bound to. Where coverage is thinner, as in Hackney, that wave would gather pace faster and wash over a larger share of the population than in a better protected place such as Cambridgeshire.
