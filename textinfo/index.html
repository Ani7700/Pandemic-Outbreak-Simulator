Verrow fever — local information

How the vaccine protects a child

If a child is exposed to a case of Verrow fever, the chance of catching it depends on how many doses they have had:

DosesChance of catching it if exposed
No dosesabout 6 in 10 (≈60%)
One doseabout 3 in 10 (≈27%)
Two dosesabout 7 in 100 (≈7%)
  • Each dose lowers the chance a lot. The first dose gives the biggest single improvement (6 in 10 → 3 in 10); the second dose lowers it further (3 in 10 → 7 in 100).
  • A child with two doses is the least likely to catch it — much less likely than an unvaccinated child.
  • Even after both doses, a small chance always remains — no vaccine is 100%.
  • Example: of 200 unvaccinated children all exposed, roughly 120 would catch it; of 100 children with both doses, only about 7 would.

If an outbreak started — the next 6 months

If Verrow fever began spreading, a model projects how the number of people infected would change over about six months. It does not stay flat: it rises to a peak and then falls again as the outbreak burns out.

  • The projection shows a most-likely path plus a range of possible outcomes around it — that range represents how uncertain the projection is (not deaths, not past cases).
  • It is a model of what could happen if an outbreak started, not a guaranteed prediction.
  • Where coverage is lower (Hackney), an outbreak would spread faster and reach a larger share of the population than where coverage is higher (Cambridge / Cambridgeshire).
